A comparative study of the Income‑tax Act, 1961 and the Income‑tax Act, 2025 — what genuinely changed, what was simply renamed, and what it means for the country living through the handover.

01 — Why re‑legislate at all

Sixty‑five years, four thousand amendments.

India's direct tax framework underwent its most significant structural overhaul in over six decades with the enactment of the Income‑tax Act, 2025 ("ITA 2025"), effective 1 April 2026, replacing the Income‑tax Act, 1961 ("ITA 1961"). Over its 65‑year life, the 1961 Act accumulated more than 4,000 amendments across 65 Finance Acts, leaving a fragmented structure of provisos, explanations, and cross‑references that made compliance difficult without professional help.

The Income Tax Department's own stated objective was a streamlined, simplified, modern code with reduced compliance burden and clear definitions — while explicitly clarifying that the new Act does not impose any new tax. That single line is the thesis of this whole project: ITA 2025 is a re‑codification of the same policy, not a policy change.

02 — Structural comparison

Same law, leaner shelf.

The most visible difference lies in size and organisation. Section count fell, yet numbering can look larger in places — ITA 2025 drops alphabetical suffixes like 80C and 115BAC in favour of plain sequential numbers, a presentational change rather than an expansion of law.

03 — Key conceptual change

"Tax Year" replaces two parallel clocks.

Under ITA 1961, taxpayers juggled the Previous Year (when income was earned) and the Assessment Year (when it was assessed) — a persistent source of confusion for new taxpayers. ITA 2025 folds both into one unified idea. Mechanically nothing changes: income is still assessed after the year ends. Only the label is simpler, and Section 536(3) confirms "tax year" reads as "previous year" for transitional purposes.

04 — Section renumbering

Nearly every citation moved. Flip a card to find it.

This is the change most likely to disrupt day‑to‑day practice: software, client communication, and legal drafting all lean on familiar section numbers. Professional commentary is unanimous on one caution — do not apply ITA 2025 numbering retrospectively. Matters relating to AY 2025‑26 and earlier must still cite ITA 1961.

05 — Continuity & transition

Section 536 keeps both Acts alive at once.

The Repeal and Savings clause provides that repealing ITA 1961 on 1 April 2026 does not disturb assessments, proceedings, or appeals relating to years before that date. In practice:

AY 2023‑24

Assessments already completed stay valid

Closed assessments under ITA 1961 are not reopened or disturbed by the repeal.

AY 2024‑25 and earlier

Pending appeals stay on the old Act

CIT(A), ITAT, High Courts and the Supreme Court continue to decide these matters under ITA 1961.

FY 2025‑26 → filed July 2026

Governed entirely by ITA 1961

Even though the return is filed after ITA 2025 is already in force, this income year is judged under the old Act.

1 April 2026

ITA 2025 comes into force

The Income‑tax Rules, 2026 replace the Income‑tax Rules, 1962 from the same date. A section‑mapping utility is released to help the transition.

Tax Year 2026‑27 → filed ~July 2027

First returns fully under ITA 2025

Income earned from 1 April 2026 onward is described as Tax Year 2026‑27 and governed by the new Act.

06 — What has not changed

The substance was never up for debate.

✓

Income tax slabs and rates, under both regimes, remain exactly as legislated under the Finance Act, 2025.

✓

The Section 87A rebate — effectively nil tax up to ₹12 lakh under the new regime — is retained, just renumbered.

✓

The ₹75,000 standard deduction for salaried taxpayers under the new regime continues unchanged.

✓

Deductions similar to 80C and 80D remain available, under Sections 122–154 in ITA 2025.

✓

The new regime stays the default; taxpayers can still opt for the old regime via Form 10‑IEA.

✓

Same judicial precedents, same legislative intent — only the section numbers encoding them have changed.

07 — Who actually feels this

Impact depends entirely on who's asking.

Limited immediate impact. Returns filed in July 2026 for FY 2025‑26 still reference familiar Sections 80C, 80D and 115BAC, and use existing forms like Form 16 and Form 26AS. The visible change ahead is vocabulary — "Assessment Year" giving way to "Tax Year" in notices and returns from FY 2026‑27.

Far more significant. CAs, tax lawyers and finance teams must update section‑reference tables, ERP and accounting software (Tally, Zoho and similar) for TDS codes, client templates and case‑management systems — while being careful never to cite ITA 2025 provisions for years still governed by ITA 1961, or vice versa.

A genuine dual‑track burden. The ITAT registry, High Courts and Supreme Court must simultaneously handle ITA 1961 appeals for pre‑April 2026 years and a growing volume of ITA 2025 matters — likely to persist for several years as older assessment years work through the system.

The reform mirrors a global shift toward simplified, principle‑based tax codes and taxpayer‑centric compliance — aligning with India's wider digital tax push: pre‑filled returns, the Annual Information Statement, and faceless assessment.

08 — Reading it critically

Housekeeping isn't free.

Cost of renumbering

Renumbering without policy change still imposes a real, non‑trivial retraining and compliance cost on software vendors, professionals and institutions — even though the underlying law hasn't moved.

Dual‑citation risk

The multi‑year window where both Acts apply to different periods raises the risk of inadvertent misciting in submissions, notices, and even judicial orders.

Litigation, unchanged

Because this is re‑codification, not policy reform, claims that it will cut litigation should be treated cautiously — the same interpretive disputes are likely to resurface under new section numbers.

Execution dependency

The simplification goal depends on CBDT and the judiciary keeping cross‑reference material current — any lag could temporarily increase reliance on external commentary, not reduce it.

09 — Conclusion

The numbers changed. The logic didn't.

ITA 2025 is a long‑overdue structural modernisation — a unified Tax Year, section consolidation, tabular presentation, and comprehensive renumbering, all improving readability without touching the substantive tax burden. Meanwhile Section 536 keeps both Acts simultaneously relevant for years: ITA 1961 for pre‑April 2026 years and pending proceedings, ITA 2025 for everything after.

A sound understanding of Indian income tax — old Act or new — ultimately rests on the same legal principles, judicial precedent, and legislative intent that gave the original provisions their meaning. The numbers may have changed; the logic they encode has not.
